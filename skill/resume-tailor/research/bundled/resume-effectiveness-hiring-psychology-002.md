## 1. Recruiter Behavior

### 1.1 The 6–10 Second Scan: Empirical Evidence

**TheLadders Eye-Tracking Studies (2012, 2018)**  
The most frequently cited research comes from TheLadders, which equipped 30 professional recruiters with eye-tracking technology over a 10-week period. Key findings:

- **2012 study**: Recruiters spent **6 seconds** reviewing an individual resume during initial screening—despite self-reported claims of 4–5 minutes per resume.
- **2018 study**: Slight improvement to **7.4 seconds**, but core scanning patterns remained consistent.
- **2025 Wonsulting experiment**: Jerry Lee’s hidden eye-tracking experiment with recruiters reinforced the F-pattern and importance of quantified achievements.

**Source**: TheLadders, "Keeping an Eye on Recruiter Behavior" (2012); TheLadders 2018 update; ResumeHeatMap synthesis; Wonsulting 2025 experiment.

**Caveats**: TheLadders research has been criticized for commercial interest, proprietary methodology, small sample size (30 recruiters), and lack of peer review. However, findings on *where* attention goes (fixation points, F-pattern, top-third dominance) are consistent across multiple studies and align with Nielsen Norman Group’s broader reading research.

### 1.2 The Six Fixation Points

Eye-tracking data identified six elements that capture **~80% of recruiter attention** during initial screening:

| Priority | Element | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| 1 | **Name** | First fixation; orients the recruiter on the document |
| 2 | **Current Job Title** | Primary relevance filter; mismatch can stop review |
| 3 | **Current Company** | Recognizable names create instant credibility (4× more attention than generic descriptions) |
| 4 | **Employment Dates** | Tenure and gap assessment; ~0.7 sec per title vs ~0.2 sec per description |
| 5 | **Previous Job Title & Company** | Career trajectory and progression |
| 6 | **Education** | Degree completion and institution; minimal attention for experienced professionals |

**Beyond these six**: Recruiters do little more than scan for keywords to match the open position—a cursory "pattern matching" activity. Detail and explanatory copy become filler with little impact on the initial yes/no decision.

### 1.3 F-Pattern and E-Pattern Reading

**Nielsen Norman Group (2006, 2017)**  
NN/G’s eyetracking research on 232 users across thousands of web pages established the F-shaped reading pattern. The same pattern applies to resume scanning:

**F-Pattern components:**
1. **Top horizontal stripe**: First read across the upper part (name, contact, headline)
2. **Lower horizontal stripe**: Shorter second pass (current job title and company)
3. **Vertical stripe**: Scan down the left side—section headers and **first 2–3 words of each line**

**E-Pattern variation**: Sometimes a third horizontal movement occurs, resembling an E rather than an F. The inverted L (one horizontal pass) is another variant.

**Implications for resumes:**
- Start subheads, paragraphs, and bullet points with **information-carrying words**—users read the third word on a line much less often than the first two.
- The first two paragraphs (or top two bullets) must state the most important information.
- Users won’t read text thoroughly; exhaustive reading is rare.

**Source**: Nielsen Norman Group, "F-Shaped Pattern For Reading Web Content" (2006, 2017 confirmation).

### 1.4 Heat Map Data: Where Attention Goes

**High-attention zones:**
- **Top-left quadrant**: Disproportionate attention; red on heat maps
- **First 2–3 words of each bullet**: Only words guaranteed to be seen during vertical scanning
- **Company names and recognizable institutions**: 4× more attention than job descriptions

**Low-attention / ignored zones:**
- Bottom third of page one: Very low
- Page two and beyond: Almost none during initial screening
- Right side of resume: Low (eye movement favors left alignment)
- Long paragraphs: Skipped
- Skills section at bottom: Minimal; often never reached in 7.4 seconds
- Objective statements: Skipped (generic content provides no value)

**Time distribution**: ~70% of review time concentrated on the top third of the page.

### 1.5 ATS Screening Before Human Review

**Multi-stage filtering:**
- **Layer 1 – Format parsing**: Eliminates ~43% of resumes (tables, text boxes, multi-column, image-based PDFs)
- **Layer 2 – Keyword matching**: Eliminates ~38%; below 70–80% match = filtered out
- **Layer 3 – AI scoring**: Eliminates ~16%; calculates likelihood-of-success score

**58% of applications** are filtered out by ATS before human review. Resumes that pass ATS are then subject to the 6–10 second human scan.

### 1.6 Differences by Role Level

| Level | Screener | Focus | Resume Strategy |
|-------|----------|-------|-----------------|
| **Individual Contributor** | ATS + junior recruiters | Keyword match, task completion, metrics | Dense bullets, tactical verbs (developed, supported), task + metric proof |
| **Manager / Director** | Mid-level recruiters, hiring managers | Team impact, scope, leadership signals | Blend of tactical and strategic; team size, cross-functional work |
| **Executive** | CEOs, board members, retained search, investors | Vision, growth potential, enterprise impact | 2–3 pages; strategic language (led, orchestrated, transformed); enterprise-scale metrics (revenue, P&L, geographic expansion); brevity signals confidence |

**Executive resume differences:**
- Reframe accomplishments as enterprise-level impact
- Use strategic verbs: led, orchestrated, transformed, drove
- Demonstrate scale: revenue influenced, workforce managed, shareholder value
- Sharper structure: leadership summary, key achievements, concise descriptions
- 2–3 pages acceptable with substance

**Source**: Career Agents, InSync Resources, ResumeFast, The Big Game Hunter.

## 2. Bullet Frameworks

### 2.1 CAR (Challenge–Action–Result)

**Structure**: Challenge → Action → Result

**When to use**: Most accomplishments; creates a mini-story in one line; ideal for initiative and problem-solving.

**Example – Weak:**
> Responsible for coordinating new hire training sessions

**Example – Strong (CAR):**
> Designed and delivered a new onboarding program that cut time-to-productivity by 30% and boosted new hire satisfaction scores by 22%

### 2.2 STAR (Situation–Task–Action–Result)

**Structure**: Situation → Task → Action → Result

**When to use**: Higher-level accomplishments, leadership scenarios, when additional context justifies why a project mattered.

**Resume application**: STAR is often used in interviews; for resumes, the Situation/Task can be compressed into a brief context clause.

**Example:**
> Led cross-functional initiative to consolidate 5 legacy CRM systems; coordinated 12 stakeholders across 3 regions, reducing integration costs by $2M and improving pipeline visibility by 40%

### 2.3 PAR (Problem–Action–Result)

**Structure**: Problem → Action → Result

**When to use**: Operations, support, technical, or administrative work; earlier roles; quick, impact-focused statements.

**Example – Weak:**
> Maintained company database and updated records

**Example – Strong (PAR):**
> Identified outdated data records and implemented a cleanup protocol that reduced database errors by 40% and improved reporting accuracy

### 2.4 Framework Selection Guide

| Framework | Best For | Bullet Length |
|-----------|----------|----------------|
| CAR | Most roles; initiative, problem-solving | 1–2 lines |
| STAR | Leadership, complex projects needing context | 2 lines |
| PAR | Technical, ops, support; concise impact | 1 line |

### 2.5 Quantification Research

**Resume-Now 18.4M Resume Analysis (2025)**  
Analysis of 18.4 million U.S. resumes (May–July 2025):

- **Only 10% of resumes** include measurable results (numbers, percentages, dollar amounts)
- **36% contain zero** measurable metrics
- **26% include five or more** quantifiable results
- **34% of recruiters** consider lack of result statements a dealbreaker

**By experience level:**
- Entry-level (0–1 years): 5% include metrics
- Early-career (1–4 years): 9%
- Mid-career (4–10 years): 11%
- Experienced (10+ years): 12%

**Callback impact**: Resumes with quantified metrics are **~40% more likely** to receive interview callbacks.

**Recommended proportion**: **~80% of bullets** should contain numbers or measurable outcomes. Industry guidance ranges from 70–90%; 80% is a robust target.

### 2.6 Metrics That Matter Most

**High-impact metrics:**
- Revenue generated or influenced
- Cost savings / cost reduction
- Time savings (%, days, hours)
- Team size managed
- Customer/user count
- Percentage improvements (conversion, efficiency, satisfaction)
- Budget size
- Geographic/scope scale

**Formula**: **Action Verb + What You Did + Quantified Result**  
Alternative: **"Accomplished [X] as measured by [Y] by doing [Z]"**

### 2.7 Action Verb Lists

**Strong verbs (use):**
- **Leadership**: Led, directed, spearheaded, orchestrated, mentored, championed, facilitated
- **Results**: Optimized, streamlined, automated, increased, decreased, accelerated, boosted
- **Creation**: Designed, engineered, formulated, implemented, launched, developed
- **Problem-solving**: Resolved, diagnosed, innovated, enhanced

**Weak verbs (avoid):**
- Worked, Made, Took, Showed, Helped
- Assisted with, Responsible for, Duties included
- Studied, Saw, Explained

**Why weak verbs fail**: They describe duties rather than achievements; "helped with" and "assisted with" imply presence without clarifying contribution. In a 6-second scan, weak verbs blur together.

**Source**: Rezi.ai analysis of 102,944 resumes; CraftYourResume; Dice.com.

### 2.8 Bullet Length Guidelines

**Standard**: **1–2 lines per bullet** (approximately 15–35 words).

- **1 line**: Clear-cut achievements with obvious impact; straightforward cause-and-effect
- **2 lines**: Complex initiatives needing context; multiple outcomes; leadership scope
- **3+ lines**: Avoid—split into two bullets

**Rationale**: Recruiters spend 6–10 seconds on initial scans; long paragraphs get skipped. ATS also processes shorter, punchy statements more efficiently.

**Structure per bullet:**
1. Strong action verb
2. Specific task or action
3. Quantified result
4. Context only if it adds value

## 3. Keyword Optimization

### 3.1 ATS Keyword Matching Thresholds

**Typical range**: 60–80% match; thresholds vary by employer and platform.

**Recommended targets:**
- **70–80%+**: Strong enough to pass automated filtering
- **75%+**: Generally reaches human reviewers
- **80%+**: Cited as minimum effective dose for modern AI screening

**Below threshold**: Resumes are automatically rejected before human review.

### 3.2 How Match Percentage Is Calculated

Modern ATS systems use:
- **Exact-match parsing** for structured fields (titles, dates, companies)
- **Semantic relevance** (NLP) comparing resume language to job description
- **Contextual scoring** based on keyword placement
- **Keyword proximity** and phrase matching
- **Skill-to-experience correlation**

**Weighting**: Keywords in achievement descriptions with metrics carry more weight than keywords in skills sections alone.

### 3.3 Natural Integration vs. Stuffing

**Keyword stuffing**: Repeating keywords excessively or unnaturally (e.g., "customer service" listed multiple times without context).

**Penalties for stuffing:**
- ~52% penalty for repetitive usage without context variation
- 41% higher rejection rates
- 67% of hiring managers discard obviously stuffed resumes

**Natural integration**: Weaving keywords into authentic achievement statements with proper context.

**Best-practice structure**: [Action Verb] + [Keyword/Skill] + [Specific Context] + [Quantified Result] + [Business Impact]

**Example – Stuffing:**
> Proficient in digital marketing, SEO, content strategy, and social media management

**Example – Natural:**
> Designed and implemented a content strategy that improved SEO rankings by 30%, driving a 20% increase in web traffic through targeted social media campaigns

### 3.4 Keyword Density

**Target**: ~2–4% keyword density rather than forced repetition.

**Distribution**: Spread keywords across professional summary, skills section, and work experience.

**Quality over quantity**: One well-placed keyword beats ten forced ones.

### 3.5 Synonym Handling

Modern ATS platforms use semantic analysis and understand:
- Skill relationships (e.g., AWS ↔ cloud infrastructure)
- Job title hierarchies
- Industry terminology

**Strategy**: Use exact job-description terms where possible, but synonyms and variations demonstrate breadth. Avoid over-repetition of the same term.

### 3.6 Job Description Parsing for Keyword Extraction

**5-step framework:**
1. Collect the full job posting
2. Identify core responsibilities
3. Spot required skills and qualifications
4. Rank keywords by frequency and relevance
5. Map keywords to experience using CAR/STAR/PAR

**Extraction methods:**
- Manual: Pull nouns, verb phrases, and required qualifications
- TF-IDF: Measure term frequency vs. inverse document frequency
- LLM-based: Extract and rank by frequency across multiple similar postings
- Gap analysis: Compare job-description keywords to resume coverage

### 3.7 Industry-Specific Keyword Banks

**TPM**: Program governance, cross-functional coordination, CI/CD, roadmap, JIRA  
**DevOps**: Terraform, Kubernetes, ArgoCD, GitOps, SRE, MTTR, cost optimization  
**Platform**: Self-service platform, developer experience, IDP, IaC  
**FinTech**: SOX, FINRA, AML/KYC, PCI-DSS, SOC 2  
**Healthcare**: HIPAA, HITECH, 21 CFR Part 11, GxP, HL7, FHIR  
**Computer/Engineering**: Highest metric inclusion (22%); Management (14%); Architecture/Engineering (12%)

## 4. Top-Third Strategy

### 4.1 What Belongs in the Top Third

The upper **30–40%** of page one carries the most weight. With 80% of resumes never shortlisted, this space is often the only chance to stand out.

**Must include:**
- Name (prominent, top-left)
- Target job title or current title
- Current company (or "Seeking [Target Title]")
- **Strongest quantified achievement**—move biggest results to the top 10%
- Specific numbers (budgets, team sizes, revenue)
- Industry-recognized terms

**Avoid:**
- Excessive contact details
- Generic phrases ("passionate," "hardworking")
- Vague objective statements
- Long summary paragraphs without metrics

### 4.2 Summary / Professional Profile Structure

**Length**: 2–4 sentences.

**Three questions to answer:**
1. What can you do for them?
2. What have you achieved? (with metrics)
3. Who are you? (role, experience level, key expertise)

**3-sentence formula:**
1. Professional title + years of experience + key expertise
2. Biggest achievement with quantifiable results
3. What you bring to the role/company

### 4.3 Target Title Placement

Place the **target job title** (or aligned current title) immediately visible—ideally in the top 10%. Recruiters use current title as primary relevance filter; mismatch can stop review.

### 4.4 Strongest Achievement Placement

**Rule**: Put your most impressive, quantified achievement in the **first bullet** of your current/most recent role. The first bullet receives the most attention during vertical scanning.

**Also**: Consider a "Key Achievement" or "Selected Achievements" highlight in the summary for executives.

### 4.5 Tailoring the Summary for Different Roles

- **Role-specific keywords**: Mirror 3–5 key terms from the job description
- **Relevant metrics**: Lead with metrics that match the role (e.g., revenue for sales, uptime for SRE)
- **Framing**: Emphasize the experience most relevant to the target role
- **Avoid**: One-size-fits-all summary; tailor per application

## 5. Section Order and Structure

### 5.1 Reverse-Chronological Format

**Standard**: Work experience from most recent to oldest.

**Preference**: Preferred by ~90% of recruiters; shows career progression clearly; most ATS-friendly.

**Typical order:**
1. Contact information
2. Resume summary
3. Skills
4. Work experience (current → oldest)
5. Education
6. Additional sections (certifications, projects)

### 5.2 When to Use Hybrid Format

**Hybrid (combination)**: Prominent skills section + reverse-chronological work history.

**Best for:**
- Senior executives
- Career changers
- Military-to-civilian transitions
- Freelancers with diverse project work

**Structure:**
1. Contact
2. Summary
3. Skills (detailed, grouped by category)
4. Work experience (reverse-chronological with dates)
5. Education
6. Additional

**Functional format**: Skills over timeline; often viewed with suspicion (can hide gaps). Use only for creative industries or when explicitly appropriate.

### 5.3 Skills Section Placement

**Place near the top**, not at the bottom.

**Rationale:**
- 75% of recruiters use ATS skill filters; missing keywords = never reach human review
- ATS scans top-to-bottom; early placement improves parser capture
- Recruiters spend &lt;15 seconds on initial review; skills must be visible early

**Content**: Hard skills, technical abilities; match job description keywords. Soft skills demonstrated in experience bullets.

### 5.4 Education Placement for Experienced Candidates

**Experienced (5+ years)**: Place education **after** work experience. Accomplishments matter more than education.

**Early-career (0–5 years)**: Education can go before or after experience depending on strength of degree/institution.

**Academic/scientific roles**: Education may be listed higher.

**Format for experienced**: Institution, degree, major; graduation date optional if many years ago. Keep concise.

### 5.5 Certifications

**When to include**: Required/preferred by posting; relevant to role; current and valid; from recognized bodies.

**Placement:**
- Separate "Licenses and Certifications" section (4+ credentials)
- In Education section (1–2 credentials)
- In header if required for role (e.g., PE, PMP)

**Tailor**: Include only certifications that strengthen candidacy for that specific role.

### 5.6 Projects

**Include when:**
- Experience section doesn’t show needed background
- Recent graduate (remove school projects after first full-time role)
- Career changer
- Project highlights skills the employer seeks

**Strategy**: Review job description; include only projects that prove required skills. Avoid overcrowding.

### 5.7 Content Volume by Role (Years of Experience → Page Count)

| Experience | Page Count | Notes |
|------------|------------|-------|
| 0–5 years | 1 page | Focused, achievement-driven |
| 5–10 years | 1–2 pages | One if focused; two if strong achievements |
| 10+ years | 2 pages | Standard for experienced |
| Executive | 2–3 pages | Acceptable with substance |

**Word count**: Resumes with **475–600 words** had ~8.2% interview rate—nearly double those outside this range. Over 600 words saw ~43% drop in callbacks. (Source: Wobo.ai)

**Principle**: Quality over length. A tight one-pager beats a padded two-pager.

## 6. Evidence-Based Rules

### 6.1 Callback Rate Studies

**Quantified bullets**: Resumes with quantified metrics **~40% more likely** to receive callbacks.

**Tailored vs. generic**: Candidates matching **70%+ of job description keywords** see **~2.5× higher** callback rates.

**Case study (CVTailor)**: Marketing professional increased callback rate from 30% (3/10) with generic resume to **70% (7/10)** with tailored resumes.

**84% of recruiters** never respond to non-customized resumes.

### 6.2 Two-Page vs. One-Page

**Overall**: Two-page resumes receive **~2.3× more callbacks** than one-page.

**By experience**: 78% of recruiters prefer one page for candidates with &lt;10 years. For entry-level, two-page advantage drops to ~1.4×.

### 6.3 Resume Quality (OpenIntro / Bertrand & Mullainathan)

**Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004)**: "Are Emily and Greg More Employable than Lakisha and Jamal?" Field experiment with 4,870 resumes; randomized resume quality and attributes.

**Finding**: Resume quality (higher vs. lower) significantly affects callback rates. The study also found large causal effects of race (white applicants ~50% higher callback) and no significant gender effect—demonstrating that resume attributes are causally linked to outcomes when properly randomized.

**Implication**: Resume quality is a lever; tailoring and optimization can materially affect callback rates.

### 6.4 Professional vs. Self-Written Resumes (TheLadders)

**Usability rating**: Professionally rewritten resumes averaged **6.2** vs. **3.9** for self-written (1–7 scale)—**60% improvement**.

**Organization/visual hierarchy**: **5.6** vs. **4.0**—**40% increase**.

**Recruiter feedback**: Re-written resumes were "easier to read," "clearer," with less data and even formatting.

### 6.5 ATS Rejection Rates

- **58%** of applications filtered by ATS before human review
- **43%** eliminated by format parsing
- **38%** eliminated by keyword matching (below threshold)
- **16%** eliminated by AI scoring layer
